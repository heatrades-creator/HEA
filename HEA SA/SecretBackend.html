How the STC calculation works
STCs = floor(systemKw × ZONE_RATING × DEEMING_YEARS)
Solar rebate value = STCs × STC_PRICE
Example: 13.3 kW × 1.382 × 5 = 91 STCs × $37.20 = $3,385 rebate. The floor() means you always round down — partial STCs are lost.

Was: 1.382

The scheme ends in 2030. Each year, this number drops by 1: 2027 = 4 years, 2028 = 3, etc. You must update this every January or the rebate amount will be wrong. A drop from 5 to 4 on a 13 kW system is about $680 less rebate.

Bill Calculation Logic
Exactly how "Before Solar" and "After Solar" bills are calculated — the most visible numbers in the app
Code.gs + Index.html
BEFORE SOLAR — Annual Bill
annBefore = annualKwh × avgTariff ÷ 100 + connFee × 365
Uses the weighted average tariff across all hours. The ÷ 100 converts cents to dollars. The supply charge ($1.034 × 365 = $377/yr) is always added — solar never reduces this. This is the "headline bill" number shown prominently in quotes.
AFTER SOLAR — Annual Bill
annAfter = gridImportKwh × avgTariff ÷ 100 + connFee × 365 − FiT income
FiT income = exportKwh × fitC ÷ 100
Only the electricity you still have to buy from the grid. FiT credits reduce the bill. Supply charge stays the same. gridImportKwh = annualKwh − selfKwh — everything not covered by solar+battery must be bought from grid.
SAVINGS = Before − After
annualSavings = selfKwh × avgTariff ÷ 100 + exportKwh × fitC ÷ 100
Two sources of savings: (1) solar power you use directly or via battery — each kWh worth avgTariff cents, and (2) solar power exported to grid — each kWh worth only fitC cents. This is why self-consumption rate matters so much: self-use is typically 7× more valuable than export (24c vs 3.3c).
Important: The avgTariff used in bill calculations is the weighted average across hours for TOU plans, or just the flat rate for flat tariffs. For flat rate at 24.112c: avgTariff = 24.112c. For TOU: avgTariff = (peak×6 + shoulder×10 + offpeak×8) ÷ 24. This affects how large the "Before Solar" bill looks.

Key Insight — Why batteries matter

24.1c ÷ 3.3c = 7.3× more valuable to self-consume
Every kWh you use from your own panels saves 24.1c. Every kWh exported earns only 3.3c. So self-consuming is 7.3× more valuable than exporting. A battery turns exported surplus into self-consumption — that's the financial case for battery storage.

How the battery simulation works (hour by hour)
If solar > load: surplus charges battery, leftover exported
If solar < load: battery discharges to cover deficit, remainder from grid
The simulation starts with SOC = 20% of capacity (not empty, not full — a realistic starting point). It runs through all 24 hours sequentially. Energy going INTO the battery is multiplied by BAT_EFF — so to store 1 kWh, you need to put in 1.087 kWh of solar. Energy coming OUT is full face value.

Generation Formula
The full chain from panel wattage to annual kWh — where the "how much power will my system make" number comes from
Code.gs
Full Generation Chain
Step 1: Get BOM irradiance data for Melbourne (24×12 matrix, W/m²·hr)
Step 2: Apply zone multiplier → irr[zone] = irr[mel] × ZONE_MULT
Step 3: Group into seasons (average 3 months per season)
Step 4: Gen per kW [Wh/hr] = irr[hour][season] × systemKw × efficiency
Step 5: Extrapolate: annualGen = Σ (genPerHour × seasonDays × weekdayWeighting)
The irradiance matrix is from BOM BERS Table 4.5, north-facing panels at latitude tilt — the optimal angle for Victoria. The "1,400 solar work hours" rule of thumb comes from summing this matrix: Melbourne base ≈ 1,400 productive hours/kWp/year. Bendigo (NORTH zone, +6%) gets ≈ 1,484 hours.

Self-Consumption Rate Definition
What exactly "self-consumption %" means in this app — and why it matters
Code.gs + Index.html
How Self-Consumption % is calculated
SCR = selfKwh ÷ genKwh × 100
where: selfKwh = kWh used directly from solar + kWh used from battery
and: genKwh = selfKwh + exportKwh (total generated)
This is: "what % of what you generate do you actually use?" — NOT "what % of your consumption comes from solar." A system that generates 8,000 kWh and uses 6,000 of it directly has an SCR of 75%, regardless of how much the home consumes.
Common confusion: A lower SCR doesn't mean the system is bad — it might just mean the system is oversized for the load. A large 20kW system on a small home will have a low SCR (lots of export) but still good savings. SCR is most useful for deciding if a battery adds value: if SCR is below 70%, a battery could capture that exported surplus.
"True SCR" vs "Solar fraction" — two different metrics
SCR (self-consumption rate) = selfKwh ÷ genKwh — what % of generation is used
Solar fraction = selfKwh ÷ annualKwh — what % of consumption comes from solar
The app shows SCR (the first formula) on the slider panel. The PDF shows both concepts. For a 10 kW system on an 8,000 kWh/yr home: if it generates 12,000 kWh and self-uses 7,200 kWh → SCR = 60%, Solar fraction = 90%. The home is 90% solar-powered, but only 60% of solar is self-consumed.

Loan Amortisation Engine
How the app converts your weekly/fortnightly/monthly repayment into an exact per-year outflow — this is the missing finance maths
Index.html — buildFinanceOutflows()
This is the engine behind "repay $50/week at 5% p.a." — it calculates exactly how many payment periods the loan lasts, then buckets those repayments into annual totals used in the DCF model. No assumed loan term — it derives it from your exact repayment amount.
Step 1 — How many periods to pay off the loan?
✏ formula
This is the standard annuity formula rearranged to solve for n.
r_period = annual_rate ÷ payments_per_year (e.g. 5% p.a. weekly = 0.05÷52 = 0.000962 per week)
Example: $10,000 loan at 5% p.a., repaying $50/week:
r = 0.05÷52 = 0.000962 | n = -ln(1 - 10000×0.000962/50) / ln(1.000962) = ~227 weeks = 4.4 years
Edge case: if rate = 0, then n_periods = Principal ÷ Repayment (simple division).
Edge case: if (Principal × r) ≥ Repayment, the loan can never be paid off — capped at analysis_years × ppy.
Step 2 — Total interest paid
✏ formula
You pay back more than you borrowed. The difference is the total interest. Example: $50/wk × 227 weeks = $11,350 total paid on a $10,000 loan = $1,350 interest. This is shown in the Finance card under "Assumptions".
Step 3 — Bucket repayments into annual totals
✏ formula
Each payment period is mapped to a year: period 1–52 → Year 1, periods 53–104 → Year 2, etc. The final partial period is prorated. These annual totals become Loan_Repayments_t in the Annual_NCF_t formula — the direct connection to the DCF engine.
Minimum repayment guard
Condition: loan can never be repaid
✏ formula
If your weekly repayment is less than the weekly interest accrual, the loan balance grows forever — you never pay it off. The app detects this and caps the period at the full analysis years. In practice this means the DCF chart will show loan repayments continuing for the full 25 years.
Payment frequency mapping
ppy = payments per year
Weekly → ppy = 52
Fortnightly → ppy = 26
Monthly → ppy = 12
✏ formula
The annuity formula needs a per-period rate, not an annual rate. A 5% annual rate becomes 5%÷52 = 0.0962% per week. This matters — weekly compounding gives slightly different results than monthly. The app uses exact per-period compounding throughout.
How this connects to Annual_NCF_t: The loan outflow array produced here feeds directly into F_t = fin_outflows[t-1] in the DCF engine. So Annual_NCF_t = Savings_t − Maintenance_t − Replacements_t − F_t (loan repayments). When the loan is paid off, F_t = 0 and NCF jumps up — that's the "CF+ Year" on the chart.

✏ formula
✏ formula

✏ formula
